# I, Claudius
*Author: Robert Graves*
*Date Logged: March, 9, 2020*
*Tags: History* 

I, Claudius was striking. Coming fresh off 1776 by David McCullough I felt at home with the writing style, and just like the early years of the American Revolution I found the Roman empire at its peak to be a very engaging and dramatic period of history. Claudius, Germanicus, Augustus, Livia, Tiberius, Caligula, and co are names and people I will carry with me favor. I was skeptical of the historical accuracy of the book, so I was constantly reading Wikipedia articles for people and events I thought were particularly dramatic, but was amazed every time at the historical accuracy of the book. 

These people got up to some BULLSHIT sometimes. Caligula making his horse a Senator and calling himself a God was probably as wild as it got, but the pettiness and shade of the whole cast can not be understated. Livia's story was all about this kind of tea - with her rampant murder and manipulation ruining many lives and almost certainly leaving the world a worse place for the rest of human existence.

Wtf kind of planet could this have been instead if my boy Germanicus had been allowed to rule instead of Tiberius. Germanicus in particular will stick with me for a long time, and I certainly do respect the person he was as well as his German rival Hemman, who I learned was the inspiration for Sigurd - Norse mythology champion and dragon slayer. 

I, Claudius isn't without a bit of bad, though. In typical white-dude-from-before-the-2010s fashion he displays a lot of unapologetic and unacceptable sexism. Some of the lines from this book def belong on r/menwritingwomen. He lingers at times too. I know some lingering comes with the territory of historical writing, but I was bored to tears at times listening to side stories about random boring senators and battles. 

My favorite part was about 30% of the way through when a young Claudius w/two old historians Pollio & Livvy, and they are discussing the virtues of recorded history, and what the point of recording history even is. Livvy thinks history is about inspiration, Pillio thinks history is about fact, and Claudius lands pretty perfectly in the middle. The main theme is quoted below.

>"Furthermore, I consider that Carthage must be destroyed" - Cato the Censor

>"For my experience as a historian is that more documents survive by chance than by intention."

>"He was always boasting of his ancestors, as stupid people do who are aware that they have done nothing themselves to boast about."

>"“It would serve the cause of the truth,” said Pollio gently. “Wouldn’t that be something?” “And if by serving the cause of truth we admit our revered ancestors to have been cowards, liars and traitors? What then?”"

>"“It’s not disillusion, sir. I see now, though I hadn’t considered the matter before, that there are two different ways of writing history: one is to persuade men to virtue and the other is to compel men to truth. The first is Livy’s way and the other is yours: and perhaps they are not irreconcilable.”"

>"Germanicus interrupted with flashing eyes: “Often, Piso, but not always. To-morrow, for instance, I shall sit with you on the appeal tribunal and we shall see whether the high ideals of youth are controlled by any obstacle at all: and whether justice to the provincials can be denied them by any incompetent, avaricious, bloody-minded sexagenarian debauchee.”"

>"To use the majesty of law for revenging any petty act of private spite is to make a public confession of weakness, cowardice and an ignoble spirit.”"
